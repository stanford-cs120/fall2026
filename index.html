Course overview

What is safe AI, and how do we make it?

CS 120 explores this question through the technical and socio-technical challenges of understanding, evaluating, and governing AI systems. We focus on the role of human input in the training process, unintended effects of AI development and deployment, and scalable evaluation for increasingly capable systems.

Through computer science research and complementary work from adjacent fields, we will examine the labor and institutions behind AI, how evaluation is defined and audited, what oversight of AI systems could look like, and what future risks we might face. Topics span data work, foundation models, and governance, with emphasis on robustness, evaluation, and auditability.

Through lectures, readings, short quizzes, and a final project, students will learn to critically assess and contribute to safe AI development. There are no official prerequisites, although familiarity with machine learning and statistics is recommended.

Working theory of the course

AI safety depends on how human inputs, data, and judgments are produced and integrated; what unintended effects arise as models amplify and recirculate them; and whether the resulting systems can be meaningfully evaluated, audited, and governed.

01

Human inputs

Who produces data and feedback? How are judgments elicited, aggregated, and translated into model behavior?

02

Unintended effects

How do models amplify, recirculate, or reshape inputs once they are embedded in products and institutions?

03

Evaluation and audit

What evidence supports safety claims, who can inspect it, and what forms of oversight are credible at scale?

Learning goals

  • Identify where assumptions, values, and human judgments enter the AI development lifecycle.
  • Analyze technical failures together with deployment context, incentives, labor, and institutional constraints.
  • Assess the validity and limitations of benchmarks, red-team exercises, interpretability methods, and audits.
  • Design or critique an evaluation, audit, or governance intervention for an AI system.

Provisional structure

Course work

Readings and short quizzes

Students should expect research papers and complementary readings from technical and socio-technical fields, accompanied by low-stakes quizzes or short responses.

Final project

The course will culminate in a final project. Possible formats include an empirical evaluation, an audit or assurance design, a data or human-feedback study, a literature synthesis, or a governance analysis.

Exact grading weights, deadlines, team-size rules, attendance policies, late-day policies, and submission links are still under review. They will be published together so that the workload and course goals are internally consistent.
